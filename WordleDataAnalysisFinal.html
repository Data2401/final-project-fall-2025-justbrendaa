Introduction

Wordle is a popular online word puzzle where players guess a five letter word within six attempts. Each guess provides feedback: letters in the correct position are highlighted in green, letters in the word but in the wrong position appear yellow, and letters not in the word are gray. Beyond being an engaging game, Wordle presents a rich dataset for analysis. Words have unique characteristics, such as vowel-consonant balance, repeated letters, rare letters, Scrabble scores, and frequency of usage.

This analysis explores these patterns across 1600+ Wordle words to uncover trends in word selection, letter placement, and other structural features. By examining the relationships between word properties, frequency, and temporal trends, this project aims to provide insights into the hidden patterns that shape the daily puzzle.

Questions to answer

  • What patterns exist in Wordles daily answers?
  • How do letter frequencies and repeated letters change across the dataset?
  • What common linguistic patterns appear in Wordle answers (prefixes, suffixes, vowel patterns)?
  • Are there temporal trends? (for example: months with more repeated letters)

Required Packages

  • ggplot2: visualizing letter frequencies, birgrams, and distributions
  • dplyr: filtering, summarizing, and transforming the data
  • tidyr: reshaping data (pivoting)
  • kableExtra: table formatting
  • gridExtra: arrange plots together neatly
  • calendR: to create monthly Wordle heatmap

Dataset Information

Description

The dataset contains official Wordle answers from 2021 to 2025, scraped from: https://wordfinder.yourdictionary.com/wordle/answers/

Each row represents one days solution.

FUN FACT

One observation Ive made while playing Wordle is that four letter words converted to five letters by adding a plural S are quite rare.

Examples of this kind of plural would be:

  • CART -> CARTS
  • BOOK -> BOOKS
  • CARD -> CARDS

This heatmap corroborates my theory: out of 1,600+ Wordle solutions only 23 words end in S. In the future, it would be interesting to add a column to my data indicating whether a word is a noun, verb, or plural in Python, which could help further test this theory.

So how is this data useful?

To put this into practice, consider the partial Wordle solution BAKE_.

Possible answers include:

  • BAKED
  • BAKER
  • BAKES

Analyzing the letter frequency for Position 5 can guide our choice.

From the data, the potential letters are D, R, and S:

  • D occurs 83 times
  • R occurs 142 times
  • S occurs 23 times

Based on this analysis, the most likely answer is BAKER.

This suggests that the puzzle selection process does not follow a noticeable monthly or seasonal trend regarding repeated letters. In other words, the generator isnt cycling through repeated-letter words in a predictable way; the placement looks random, or at least not influenced by simple time-based rules.
